The Ideal

A Catalytic Cataclysm and a Response

For future readers, with the backdrop of 2020 being the year COVID surprised everyone,... October was expected to be a time we could finally hold medium-large events like UQ Bloom again (with safety measures). This means the project was meant to be a public installation from the start. Social distancing and minimising contact are now and will continue to be a fact of life for a long time, so what could we do we make the most of no / own touch interactions in order to connect people?

StraySpace

StraySpace is (meant to be) an installation about saving 'stray' aliens. They're lost and need energy to find their way home. To give them your energy, you have to deal with perform a ritual in front of creepy, screaming windmills.

Goals

Massive Motions

Can we succeed at giving people a context to do large, wild movements, together? To me, the project is a sort of way to challenge the recent situation where people are inside, confined and alone. Now that they're already outside, why not make them make full use of the space and everyone's presence?


Collective Movement / Collaboration

Collaborative playfulness, people work together to achieve a goal - what kind of groups dynamics appear? Do any roles or tendencies form?


Weathering Discomfort to Protect the Helpless

Motivating people to help the adorable aliens, even when faced with these creepy, scary windmills screaming at them - but taking measures not to accidentally traumatise people.


The Uncompromised StraySpace

Priorities for an installation that had no limits. I have a feeling I gradually toned down the installation as I collaborated with my teammates, to accomodate their interests. But if I could, this is how extreme I would make it.

Types of Motion

Different kinds of motions and full-body gestures trigger different groups of sensors, eliciting one of dozens of different screams from the installation.

Stress of Teamwork

Every alien saved increases the games difficulty. Failing means seeing the aliens explode in a rather intense fashion.

Scale & Intensity of Eerieness

Three giant windmills (at least two meters tall) stare the users down, and the machines themselves are located in a dark room to increase the intensity of creepyness. The aliens produce sound cues indicating their presence even if they're not on screen (with rattling metal, shaking sounds, cloth waving around, chirps and sound effects).




The Actual

The Crushing Embrace of Reality, What Lied Beyond the End

How things ended up being on the day of the exhibit. Naturally not everything was perfect.


Form

The installation consisted of three windmill boxes, suspended on pillars, with drills working as motors to spin foam blades. The pillars were those of the bridge leading to the UQ Lakes Bus Stop. The drills were controlled by motor drivers (except for one windmill that had a relay instead) that received instructions from Arduino Leonardos.

Whenever someone moved (danced) nearby the windmills, a passive infrared sensor would detect their motion, while an ultrasonic sensor would check how far they were.

The arduinos also connected to a mac mini, which hosted a game made in unity. The game was projected onto dark fabric

Function

Depending on the distance and how much the users moved, the windmills would spin in patterns and have the LED's glow in either a red, or rainbow colour. If the "correct" dance was performed and the windmill became rainbow coloured, depending on the windmill one of the aliens would stop falling, or the portal door would open. When two aliens met at the open portal, there was a chime that indicates they were saved and had reached home.

If the user was too close, or if they stopped moving, the aliens fall downwards. Until they were saved the aliens keep falling, producing a scream. Sad music played in the background on a loop.

Process & Build

Scattered Pieces Strewn Apart, Fragments of a Whole

Some of the more interesting things I did to help achieve those goals - focusing only on my own perspective of the project of course. This section isn't entirely chronological, as many tasks tended to be simultaneous. or had to be reapproached multiple times.

Part 1 : Initial Exploration

Getting the Idea Out There

Before most actual development started, I wanted to work on making sure I had a clear understanding of the concept first. This may seem counterintuitive since the idea I worked on was partially my own, but an important question I think everyone should be able to answer at any time is "what or who is this for".

Mini Compositions

To make sure I could always answer that, I started with compositions that mixed sketches, memes, and pictures . These were only for myself, and generally lived on my journal, someone else looking at these would likely interpret them differently than I would. But they were useful for giving myself a strong impression of the feelings or reactions I was aiming for, so that I could explain what I wanted to do to other people without too much pausing in our conversations.

Doodles

Doodles helped quite a bit too, quick "napkin sketches" (although I did mine digitally) used to prepare for bodystorming, or record useful movements, stances and poses. The examples I'm using here are from my project inspiration however, because they show better contrast than the doodles I made for the actual installation. I also made a number of storyboards, but those will be further explained later.

Mini Windmill

Ignoring the site visit and research that everyone needed to do - I made a mini windmill using random bits and pieces stolen from the workshop. This wasn't really useful as an actual part of the installation, but as a quick, ten minute effort to see what I would need, it helped in identifying the key components - a wide base / foundation, a tower/ pillar, a motor to spin the windmill, a centerpiece to hold blades, and the blades themselves.

Reflecting For Next Time

Drafting and planning for the windmill (the major physical bit) should have been a team effort. Because I handled early planning and drafting alone, I think it established a precedent that I had the physical aspect under control, which was rarely true, and might have hampered how complete our prototype was later in the process. In week 8, our prototypes ended up consisting of several sub-prototypes, rather than everyone's collective work all connected as one prototype.

Part 2 : Moving Matters

Where the Action is At

Initially I felt that the biggest draw of the installation would have been the scale and having the windmills spin and scream at you, a sort of surreal experience because unlike the software I'm used to working with, it was possibly the chance to create a physical presence that confronts users (or rather, gives them feedback). Here's some of the work that went into ... the spinny bit.

Triangular Tribulation

Regardless of what I made the blades from, the way they were attached needed to be secure. They also had to be fairly precisely made, to avoid wobbling windmills. This mostly involved a lot of measurements and guidelines on the markings I made for the latter three triangles (the fourth was a backup).

A triangular centerpiece would fit on a threaded rod, which would be inserted into a drill. The final version had one lock nut on the front of each blade, and two regular nuts behind, which prevented the center from sinking into and damaging the acrylic.

Zombie Brain Drill & Reflection on A Major Failure

For context, with a lot of guidance from Clay, I had a drill that could connect to an arduino, and inside it where the trigger used to be, we had a relay. Later on, the relay was replaced with a motor driver, and even later on, I had a motor driver for every drill.

At least, that was the plan. Much later in the semester I took for granted the location of a pin, assuming that "the rightmost pin is always the one that connects to power". However, for one of the drills, the pins were inverted, so the leftmost one was power - my mistake here being not looking at the label on the motor drivers actual board before wiring in the cable. This caused a small fire and burned both the motor driver, and the brand new Arduino Leonardo connected to it. To avoid a repeat of this, from that point forward I always had comparison pictures of what wire goes where stored on my phone, as well as triple checking the board before bringing the arduino over.

Music Machine

To see the limits of the motor driver, I started playing around with programming them through arduino, with the videos to the right being examples of what I managed to do. By commanding the drill to spin at specific power values, I could get it to produce specific 'notes'... using the drills motor spinning almost like an instrument. So, if I asked it to spin fast (150 power) and then immediately spin slow in the other direction, (10 power) It would be like doing a high pitched note, and a low pitched note. I could also control the duration the "note" was held

The next step was making the drills run specific combinations of powers (so, specific speeds) depending on the distance, as seen in the second video. Admittedly, at first I only tested out a bunch of ranges I expected people to interact at, and gave each of the seven a colour and corresponding speed. Had I developed this further, perhaps I could almost have given... a different personality for each windmill perhaps. Like one that always starts out with aggressive, short notes, another that does long, sustained notes, and so on.

This is probably one part I'd most be willing to return to. I really like manufactured experiences that make me feel like I'm standing at the precipice of madness, and one way to achieve that would be to make robots talking at a person in some sort of invisible, Machine language. This was unfortunately another case of me deprioritising something interesting, to work on what was necessary instead, as at that point the physical construction was more important. But I do feel like refocusing at this point meant losing some of that... spicyness , the edge in the teeth, that made the initial idea something I really wanted to pursue.

Part 3 : Every Little Thing in It's Place

Making Sure All the Scattered Pieces Fit in One Box

I spent the most amount of time through the course on this, the remainder of the physical form of the installation at various stages, through a number of different iterations of the concept. A great opportunity to try out a number of pieces of technology, and improve my handiwork.

Drafting and Preparation

Everything up to this point had been familiar territory, but the Windmill Tower, or Base, the part that supports the main moving bit, was new, especially at the two meter scale I initially wanted.

The scale was a guarantee that the pieces would need to be machined. I started with making models in tinkerCAD, since UQInnovate 3d prints pieces. This would allow me to start with an early partial scale model. In hindsight, if I went back now I would tell myself to immediately go for full scale and save a weeks worth of time fiddling with tinkerCAD, which had a lot of limitations with how shapes were constructed, aside from a variety of quality of life issues.

I then switched to Autodesk Fusion360 instead - which had it's own learning code, but when integrated with slicer3D, it was an ideal tool for discovery, much like trying to find the right jigsaw puzzle piece shapes to fit. In this case, I'd just need to make a general shape in Fusion, import that to slicer, decide how that is going to placed onto a piece of material for cutting, and then export it as a dxf file to lazer cut or possibly throw onto the Computer and Numerical Control / CNC Machine.

At that point I got feedback indicating that we should scale down our project and place it elsewhere, so instead I started working on the new form, which was meant to be a housing, or box, rather than a tower. Suspended rectangles are considerably easier to design compared to conical housings, so I used draft illustrations instead.

What's in the Booooox?

With Clay's help and a panel saw, we could ensure a straight cut on all our boards, without any odd angles or the need to slowly feed the material into the blade like with a bandsaw. I focused on assembly next, but there's not much exciting about that - each section needed at least two 3 cm screws any time it was connected to another section.

We also needed a way to secure the boxes, so I cut and bent some threaded rods into U-shapes slightly larger than the pillars we'd be attaching the box to, then filed down the edges, to avoid cross threading.

The next step was what went inside the boxes - a shelf placed about a third of the height inside, supported by two wood off cuts secured to both the side and back. These shelves supported the drills, and had holes in them that allowed us to ziptie them down. To make sure that these shelves were at level height, I used a bubble level to double check every piece inside the box. In hindsight, I should have done this for every piece of the boxes in general - because I didn't, I missed the fact that one of the boxes had a top piece 5 mm larger than the rest of the top pieces, which resulted in a crooked box.

Four other off cuts were used for each box, two close to the top, and two close to the bottom. These had predrilled holes for screws, so every time we wanted to install our acrylic panels to contain everything inside, we'd nust need to replace those.

Part 3A : Striking Impressions

Selling the Illusion, Small Efforts to Enhance the Pageantry

I believe in the possibility of making or breaking interest and interactions with the installation, before someone even approaches the site. This section talks about some of the things I consider small but meaningful, that had the potential to influence whether someone would participate.

Emotion Factory

As an additional task during one of the weekends of downtime, I started with music development. Part of the identity of the installation early on was meant to be the impression the windmills were "screaming" at you, so the first round of music (check out the soundcloud section below) was focused on finding that "scream" . Not something immediately unnerving like a real person's scream, but mechanical, alien or robotic sounds instead. Since our aliens were coming in from a different dimension, it made sense the music and any sound cues was supposed to give that impression as well.

Around that time I formally developed the storyboard as well - which had a few purposes. It served as a sort of checkpoint to see where every teammate had gone during their exploration, and helped us figure out what was left to do. But most importantly, it gave an idea of what a start to finish interaction might look like, with more specifics. One major weakness though, was neglecting to consider that storyboards work best when theres several scenarios, whereas the one I made only had two images diverging from the path. This is a mistake I'd made before, but in trying to rush in with the main scenario, I neglected Including more detail in the individual frames.

Outcomes

In the End, Did It Even Matter?

Considerations of how well it all went - or rather, how well it didn't go. The comparison between our objectives and what we actually had was ... fascinating.

The Bright Eyes Of the Public

Observations of Use of the Installation

Fascinatingly, the installation was most well received by parents with their children. Children themselves tended to be the most wild with their movements, often moving well beyond the threshold we expected, partially because their small size made it difficult for them to trigger the sensors consistently. Many parents would supervise their children, but eventually get so enthralled that they too would participate in the installation, mirroring or just guiding their children, sometimes by holding their hands.

Couples also frequently tried out the installation together, often right as another group was taking off, wed have couples coming in to take over two windmills and continue saving the aliens. This tended the case for ones who initially started out observing however - it wasnt as common to see people who just stumbled by immediately participate.

Groups of friends were the final major participants, there was a sort of jovial and unconstrained attitude amongst people there with their friends. The dynamics werent always the same, some people were too shy to potentially embarrass themselves while their peers were around, while others treated being the fool as something that brightens the mood. This isnt to say this was planned or intended necessarily, but it could be an avenue for future development, possibly with more points of input than just the three we already have.

It was relatively rare to see lone individuals come up to participate in the installation, although because the installation was at the end of the day, its likely that most people passing by alone had a purpose in mind when walking by, whereas groups of people didnt necessarily need an aim or goal to hang out, making them more likely to want to try something unusual like our installation.

I had simply expected the publics reaction to mirror my own when interacting with similar installations... that of confusion, puzzlement, and visceral recoil, so to see such frequent and consistent engagement with the installation does mean that perhaps I should reevaluate how much of the reaction I expected was just a reflection of how I would react, rather than reality.

Dark Dreams Disappear

Aspirations for Dark Desire Fade Away, to Reveal Only Bland Porridge

Compared to the ideal, the actual installation had a few clear differences - for one the scale of the installation was smaller and more compressed, although that in itself didnt stop it from drawing attention - the sounds the installation produced, especially the sound of the aliens screaming, drew plenty of attention which was self sustaining, because after the first round of participants were drawn in, successive rounds of observers would also gain an interest in the installation. With minimal lulls through the night.

Ive also become aware that over the semester, Ive gradually been toning down and simplifying the idea to make it easier to communicate with my teammates - especially because some teammates have different grasps of English than others, which made conveying even basic changes to the concept become half hour slogs. It simply got too tedious trying to keep everyone on track and re-explaining things. The issue from there is that my original goals of engaging with negative emotions and designing to challenge peoples perceptions had to be cut back on so extremely, that that aspect is almost non-existent.

I think in many small ways, Ive inadvertently killed the parts of the installation I was most interested in working on - particularly since I spent so much time on the physical build, that Ive ended up neglecting the emotional goals of the installation in various ways.

For example, the aliens were initially supposed to cry out for help, but because someone hadnt completed their share of the work, myself and other teammates had to handle both our existing tasks, and take over the unfinished work, leaving no time to work on quality of life features like the sounds the aliens were meant to make. As a result, when the aliens fade away, the impact of their disappearance is less intense. This might have been partially mitigated through the use of stock assets, but stock assets dont achieve nearly the intended experience as much as something purpose built could.

Another change was that the windmills, that were meant to produce sound cues, are instead silent except for the sound of their motor - which limits how expressive they could be. Theyre no longer screaming windmills, just mildly humming windmills instead.

Truth to Theme

Does This Really Meet the Brief Anymore?

While at first I would have said we achieved an acceptable result, the extreme frequency of people grabbing onto the foam blades of the windmill meant that if COVID restrictions were tighter, we would essentially have failed. Despite warnings everywhere, including on top of the installation boxes themselves, that youre ant to perform actions, plenty of children and even adults were so curious about getting the moving bit to spin that theyd simply latch on to the blades and try to force the interaction.

On one hand, its great they were enthusiastic, but on the other, the fact that we had to babysit the installation the entire time, and that people didnt manage to always figure out the context based on their own investigation or adherence to the rules, means its probably back to the drawing board in terms of how were going to keep the installation a no-touch focused interaction.

On the front of human connection I have mixed feelings - to me the goal was that the installation could be a place to spark connections and build a sense of collaborative play but I didnt plan for what happens after that, or the kind of followup people were ideally supposed to have. Perhaps if we were being loose with the term it could be said that by sharing the space and doing the same weird things, people were connecting, but if sharing a space and doing the same things are all it takes to form a connection, wouldnt that be like saying were all stuck in traffic together right now, so were all connected?

Abandoned Strays

Aliens Who?

Two of the major success criteria we chose earlier on were at least twenty pairs of saved aliens and the installation is active at least 75% of the day, both of which were achieved but I would say that although there were brief instances of people from separate family groups interacting, most of the time the effort was a passive one. It was quite rare to see complete strangers communicate with each other as well, and group dynamics in general didnt really form as intended.

This could possibly be attributed to the drop in, drop out nature of the installation, anyone can join in and help out, if they wanted to. However, most of the time it seems that participants treated achieving the rainbow colours an indication that they had succeeded, rather than that they were saving aliens. The instant gratification is just as good as the long term reward... or probably a better take would just be that our aliens haven't really succeeded at drawing in peoples empathy.

I would go so far as to say that the vast majority of participants didnt really care about the aliens, as even if they noticed them, they would rarely make saving any a priority. I think its because the disincentive for allowing the aliens to fall wasnt strong enough, but its likely also an issue of a lack of solid limits, because its an endless loop, theres not enough reason to get invested in the current set of aliens over any others.
